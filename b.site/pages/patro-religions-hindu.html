Hinduism

Hinduism is one of the oldest religions of the world. Hinduism is the third largest religion in the world only falling behind Christianity and Islam. The southern part of Asia, specially the Nepalese and Indian people are Hindus mostly. Majority of people here are Hindus with the heritages, sites and temples of the Hindu religion in almost every place. With different religious sites and temples built with the name of the gods and their deeds, every place/city in these two countries has a Hindu temple. It is said that there are about 33 million gods and goddesses that are worshipped by the Hindus around the globe. The wind, water, sun, moon, etc. and every other aspect are believed to be the gods and goddesses by the Hindus. The god Brahma is believed to be the creator of universe and life. The god Vishnu serves as the maintainer of the universe and life whereas the god Shiva is believed to be the destroyer.

Origin

It is believed that the Hinduism was brought to India by the invaders of Indus Valley who settled along the banks of the Indus river known as Aryans. Whereas, many scholars and pundits believe that Hinduism developed as a religion among the people living in the Indus valley since before the Iron age. There are artifacts which date to before 2000 BCE. Similarly, the word Hindu, is derived from the name of the river Indus. The river was called Sindhu in the ancient times. The pre-Islamic Persians who sailed and travelled to India called the river Hindu and the land Hindustan. The people living there were recognized as Hindus.

Deities (Gods & Goddesses)

There are about 33 million gods and goddesses believed by the Hindu religion. Every aspect of life and death are believed to be god or goddess. The sun is believed to be the god Surya, the moon as the god Chandra, etc. Among the 33 million gods, some of the most worshipped gods and goddesses are:

  • Brahma: The creator of world and the living things.

  • Vishnu: The protector and preserver of the world.

  • Shiva: The destroyer of the world for recreation.

  • Krishna: The god of love, sympathy and kindness.

  • Hanuman: The god of strength and energy as well as devotion and selfless dedication.

  • Lakshmi: The goddess of wealth and purity.

  • Saraswati: The goddess of knowledge.

  • Durga: The goddess warrior protecting the good from the evil.

Festivals

There are a large number of festivals and sacred days that are observed by the Hindus. The festivals are celebrated remembering the events that took place in Hindu mythology. As there are numerous gods and goddesses, the festivals and sacred days are more too. Each of the sacred day and festivals have their own importance and event that happened in the Hindu mythology. Some of the well-known festivals are given below.

  • Dashain: The main festival of Hindus celebrated representing the victory of good over evil. It lasts for 15 days.
  • Tihar: The festival of lights celebrated by Hindus, Sikhs, Jains and Buddhists as well. It also represents the victory of good over evil and knowledge over ignorance. It lasts for 5 days.
  • Holi: The festival of colors celebrated representing the arrival of Spring after winter. It also represents the victory of good over evil and a day of spreading happiness and love.
  • Janmashtami: The festival celebrated by the Hindus representing the birth day/tithi of god Krishna.
  • Mahashivaratri: The festival celebrated representing the birth day of god of gods Shiva.

Holy Books

There are many books in Hinduism that are sacred. The Vedas, Bhagavad Gita, 18 Mahapuranas, Ramayana and Mahabharata are the sacred books in Hinduism. The Vedas are made up of 4 sacred books. These books have the collection of verses, chants and revelations in Sanskrit. Bhagavad Gita is considered to be the holy book among these books. Ramayana embarks the journey of god Ram along with his brother god Laxman and his victory over the demon Ravan. Similarly, Mahabharata is the story that depicts the war of the 5 Pandavas and 100 Kauravas known as Kurukshetra War.
